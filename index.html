Physics has a secret

But it needs 26 numbers — the mass of the electron, the strength of electromagnetism, the Higgs field energy — that can't be calculated. They have to be measured and typed in by hand.

The language of forces

Each force is described by a symmetry group. Electromagnetism uses U(1) — rotations in a circle. The weak force uses SU(2) — rotations of a sphere. The strong force uses SU(3) — rotations in an 8-dimensional colour space. Gravity uses SO(3,1) — the symmetry of spacetime itself.

The Standard Model is the statement that SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) describes all non-gravitational physics. It works perfectly. But it doesn't explain why these groups and not others. It doesn't explain their coupling strengths. And it says nothing about gravity.

Each force has a coupling constant — a single number that sets its strength. The electromagnetic coupling is α ≈ 1/137. The weak coupling gives sin²θ_W ≈ 0.231. The strong coupling is α_s ≈ 0.118. The Standard Model measures these numbers. It cannot derive them.

The Higgs Field

Why particles have mass

The problem mass creates

Without the Higgs field, every particle would travel at the speed of light and weigh nothing. There would be no atoms, no chemistry, no structure. The weak force would have infinite range, identical to electromagnetism. The universe would be a featureless soup of massless particles.

The Higgs field fills all of space. Particles that interact with it are slowed down — and that resistance to acceleration is what we call mass. The stronger the interaction, the heavier the particle. The photon doesn't interact at all, so it remains massless. The top quark interacts maximally, so it's the heaviest.
